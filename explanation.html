How This Tool Works

Field definitions, scoring logic, and project outcome visualisation

Field Definitions (Backlog & User Ratings)

1. Requirement

A short description of a user story or system requirement. Example: “As a user, I want to reset my password so that I can regain access if I forget it.” This is the item we are trying to prioritise in the product backlog.

2. Importance (1–5)

How important this requirement is from the user’s or stakeholder’s perspective:

  • 1 – Not important (nice to have, little impact)
  • 2 – Low importance
  • 3 – Moderately important
  • 4 – Important
  • 5 – Very important / critical

Higher importance means the requirement matters more to users.

3. Value (1–5)

How much business or user value the requirement will deliver if implemented:

  • 1 – Very low value
  • 2 – Low value
  • 3 – Moderate value
  • 4 – High value
  • 5 – Very high / strategic value

Higher value means the requirement contributes more to benefits such as satisfaction, revenue, or impact.

4. Effort (1–5)

Estimated effort needed to implement this requirement (time, complexity, resources):

  • 1 – Very low effort (can be done quickly)
  • 2 – Low effort
  • 3 – Medium effort
  • 4 – High effort
  • 5 – Very high effort (complex or time-consuming)

In this tool, higher effort slightly reduces the overall priority score.

5. MoSCoW Category

MoSCoW is a common Agile prioritisation technique:

  • Must – Essential; must be delivered for the product to be acceptable
  • Should – Important, but not absolutely critical in the first release
  • Could – Nice to have; can be delivered if there is extra capacity
  • Won't – Not planned for this release (or explicitly excluded)

In the scoring model, stronger categories (Must, Should) have higher weights, helping critical requirements rise to the top.

6. Score

The Score is a combined priority value calculated from Importance, Value, Effort, and MoSCoW. Conceptually:

  • Higher Importance → increases the score
  • Higher Value → increases the score
  • Higher Effort → slightly reduces the score
  • Stronger MoSCoW category (Must / Should) → increases the score

The score makes it easier to compare requirements and sort them from highest to lowest priority in the backlog.

“Calculate & Sort Priorities” – What Happens?

When you click “⚙️ Calculate & Sort Priorities” on the main page:

  • The tool reads all user ratings (Importance, Value, Effort, MoSCoW) for each requirement.
  • It calculates a priority score for every requirement using the combined weighting logic.
  • It sorts the backlog so that the highest-scoring requirements appear at the top.
  • The top 3 items are highlighted as recommended for the next sprint or iteration.
  • At the same time, summary metrics are prepared for the Project Outcome Visualisation page.

This reflects the research idea: user involvement → structured input → improved and more transparent requirement prioritisation.

Project Outcome Visualisation – Interpretation

On the outcome page, the tool uses the top requirements (for example, the top 5 items) to estimate how your current prioritisation may affect project performance across three dimensions:

1. User Value Alignment

Indicates how well the top requirements reflect user-rated Importance and Value. Higher values suggest stronger alignment with what users consider important.

2. Delivery Efficiency

Reflects how efficient it is to deliver the top requirements, based on their average Effort. Lower-effort top items result in a higher efficiency score.

3. Criticality Coverage

Shows how many “Must” requirements are included near the top of the backlog. Higher coverage means critical items are being prioritised appropriately.

Together, these three scores form an illustrative “project outcome profile” based on the current prioritisation decisions.

How This Supports the Research

This prototype operationalises the research idea from the title: “Enhancing Requirement Prioritisation through User Involvement in Agile Projects: Implications for Project Performance.”

  • Users and stakeholders actively rate requirements (user involvement).
  • The tool converts these ratings into priority scores (enhanced prioritisation).
  • The outcome page visualises estimated project performance implications across user value, efficiency, and criticality coverage.

This can be used in your research to discuss how structured user involvement may improve requirement prioritisation and project outcomes in Agile environments.
